## Part 1: Identify Your AI Needs

### Do You Actually Need an AI Hire?

Before you post a job, ask yourself:

- **Are we solving a real problem with ML/AI?** Not every problem needs AI. (ChatGPT is not a strategy.)
- **Do we have data?** ML requires signal. Without data, you're hiring for aspirations.
- **Can we evaluate success?** If you can't measure impact, you can't retain the person.

### AI Role Assessment Matrix

| Role | Focus | Key Skills | Typical Salary (US) |
|------|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **ML Engineer** | Production systems, scalability | MLOps, Systems design, DL frameworks | $180K-280K |
| **Data Scientist** | Analysis, modeling, experimentation | Statistics, Python, SQL, communication | $150K-250K |
| **AI Research Engineer** | Novel approaches, papers, cutting-edge | Deep learning, math, research skills | $200K-400K |
| **AI Product Manager** | AI product strategy, go-to-market | Product sense, AI literacy, domain expertise | $160K-240K |
| **AI/ML Manager** | Team leadership, roadmap | Leadership, technical depth, hiring | $180K-300K |

### Red Flags in AI Hiring

- 🚩 "We want AI but don't know what for"
- 🚩 "We need someone to compete with OpenAI"
- 🚩 "Budget is tight, but we want a senior ML Engineer"
- 🚩 "The last AI hire didn't work out" (without understanding why)
- 🚩 "We'll figure out the project after we hire"

### Green Flags

- ✅ You have a specific, measurable problem
- ✅ You have data or a clear path to collect it
- ✅ Leadership understands the 6-12 month ramp time
- ✅ You have resources for tools, compute, and infrastructure
- ✅ You can articulate success in business terms, not just technical

## Part 2: Source & Screen Top Talent

### Where to Find AI Talent

**High-Signal Sources:**
- Direct sourcing via LinkedIn (focus on specific skills, not titles)
- AI/ML communities (Kaggle, Papers with Code, Hugging Face)
- University partnerships & research labs
- Referrals from existing team members
- Executive recruiters specializing in AI

**Medium-Signal Sources:**
- General job boards (AngelList, LinkedIn Jobs)
- University recruiting fairs
- Tech conferences (NeurIPS, ICML, etc.)

**Low-Signal Sources:**
- "AI experts" on Twitter with no code
- Resume keyword matches alone
- Consulting firms without hands-on experience

### Quick Screening Questions

Before the first call, use these questions to disqualify:

1. **"Tell me about a model you built end-to-end, from problem to production."**
   - Listen for: Data collection, feature engineering, evaluation, monitoring, failures learned
   - Red flag: Only academic or toy projects

2. **"What's a time you optimized a slow ML pipeline? Walk me through it."**
   - Listen for: Specific bottlenecks, trade-off thinking, measurement
   - Red flag: "I don't remember" or vague answers

3. **"Why are you interested in this role/company?"**
   - Listen for: Genuine interest in problem, team, or mission
   - Red flag: Only interested in salary or title

4. **"What's your experience with [specific tool/framework your company uses]?"**
   - Listen for: Honest "I don't know it, but I learn frameworks quickly"
   - Red flag: Claimed expertise but can't answer basic questions

### Portfolio Evaluation Rubric

When reviewing portfolios/GitHub:

| Signal | What to Look For | Weight |
|--------|-----------------|--------|
| **Project Scope** | Real-world scale, production mindset | 30% |
| **Code Quality** | Clean, documented, testable | 20% |
| **Experimentation** | Proper validation, ablations, analysis | 25% |
| **Communication** | Clear writeups, reproducibility | 15% |
| **Recency** | Current with modern frameworks | 10% |

## Part 3: Evaluate Real Capability

### The Take-Home Project

Instead of whiteboarding, give a real problem:

**Sample Project Brief:**
"Here's a dataset of customer churn. Build a model to predict which customers will churn in the next 30 days. We care about both recall (finding at-risk customers) and false positives (avoiding annoying false alerts). You have 4-6 hours. Submit code + a 10-minute walkthrough video."

**What You Learn:**
- How they approach ambiguous problems
- Code quality under time pressure
- Communication & clarity
- Practical ML thinking (not just theory)

**Rubric for Evaluation:**
- ✅ Problem framing (did they ask clarifying questions?)
- ✅ Data exploration (baseline understanding)
- ✅ Modeling approach (reasonable choices)
- ✅ Evaluation (appropriate metrics)
- ✅ Code (clean, documented)
- ✅ Insights (what did they learn?)

### System Design Interview

For senior roles (ML Engineer, Staff level):

"Design the ML pipeline for [your use case]. What would you prioritize? What tradeoffs would you make?"

Listen for:
- End-to-end thinking (data → model → serving → monitoring)
- Scalability concerns (latency, throughput, cost)
- Operational maturity (monitoring, retraining, failure modes)
- Pragmatism (shipping fast vs. perfect)

## Part 4: Onboard & Deploy

### 90-Day Ramp Checklist

**Week 1-2: Foundation**
- [ ] Team introductions, culture/values walkthrough
- [ ] Technical environment setup (dev machine, access, tools)
- [ ] Codebase tour with senior engineer
- [ ] First small task (bug fix or small feature)
- [ ] 1:1 with manager (clarify expectations, concerns)

**Week 3-4: First Real Project**
- [ ] Assigned to a scoped project (2-4 weeks, well-defined)
- [ ] Paired with a mentor for daily standup
- [ ] Weekly manager check-ins
- [ ] Encouraged to propose 1 idea/process improvement
- [ ] Team social (lunch, offsite, etc.)

**Month 2: Deeper Integration**
- [ ] Lead own project deliverable
- [ ] Present findings to stakeholders
- [ ] Own 1-2 ongoing responsibilities
- [ ] Pair with different team members
- [ ] Feedback from multiple colleagues

**Month 3: Contribution & Planning**
- [ ] Independent project ownership
- [ ] Presenting work to leadership
- [ ] 360 feedback + manager alignment
- [ ] Q4 planning / next quarter goals
- [ ] Decision: Confirm fit or pivot

### First Project Selection

✅ **Choose projects that are:**
- High-impact but not mission-critical
- Well-scoped (2-4 weeks)
- Paired with a mentor/buddy
- Have clear success metrics
- Expose new hire to real systems

❌ **Avoid:**
- "We don't know what to work on yet"
- Greenfield projects (too much ambiguity)
- Urgent firefighting (no time to learn)
- Completely isolated work (no collaboration)

## Part 5: Unlock ROI

### Project Prioritization Matrix

Score projects on:

| Factor | Scoring |
|--------|---------|
| **Business Impact** | 1-10 (revenue, cost, retention, etc.) |
| **ML Readiness** | 1-10 (data availability, problem clarity) |
| **Team Execution** | 1-10 (team skills, time available) |
| **Time to Value** | 1-10 (how quickly to ROI?) |

**Focus:** Projects scoring 30+ with strong ML Readiness.

### AI Team Structure Examples

**Startup (1-3 AI People):**
```
CEO/CTO
├─ ML Engineer (full-stack, shipping focus)
└─ Data Engineer (pipelines, infrastructure)
```

**Growth Stage (5-10 People):**
```
VP Engineering
├─ ML Engineering Lead
│  ├─ ML Engineer (systems/infra)
│  ├─ ML Engineer (product/features)
│  └─ ML Engineer (junior/rotational)
├─ Data Lead
│  ├─ Data Engineer
│  └─ Analytics Engineer
└─ Product Manager (AI)
```

**Enterprise (20+ People):**
```
Chief AI Officer
├─ ML Platform & Infrastructure
│  ├─ MLOps Engineering Lead
│  ├─ ML Infrastructure Engineers
│  └─ Compute/Data Platform Team
├─ AI Product & Features
│  ├─ Product Management
│  ├─ ML Engineers (domain-specific)
│  └─ ML Researchers
└─ Data & Analytics
│  ├─ Data Engineering Lead
│  ├─ Analytics Engineering
│  └─ Data Stewardship
```

### Measuring Impact

Define success metrics **before** starting projects:

- **Business Metrics:** Revenue lift, cost savings, user retention, NPS
- **ML Metrics:** Accuracy, precision, recall, F1, AUC-ROC
- **Operational Metrics:** Inference latency, throughput, model retraining frequency
- **Engagement:** User adoption, A/B test results

Track all four, not just accuracy.

```

ML Engineer → ML Tech Lead → ML Architect → Chief AI Officer
```

### What Actually Retains AI Talent

**Top 5 Reasons AI Engineers Stay:**
1. ✅ **Interesting problems** – They chose AI to work on hard problems
2. ✅ **Autonomy** – Trust to make technical decisions
3. ✅ **Growth opportunity** – Clear path to leveling up
4. ✅ **Great team** – Collaborating with smart people
5. ✅ **Mission alignment** – Believing in what the company does

**Top 5 Reasons They Leave:**
1. ❌ No clear ROI (their work never ships)
2. ❌ Non-technical management (can't understand their work)
3. ❌ Stalled growth (same role, no progression)
4. ❌ Toxic team dynamics
5. ❌ Better opportunity elsewhere (compensation or scope)

### Red Flags for Retention Risk

- No promotion in 2+ years
- Skipped in key decisions
- Compensation not reviewed annually
- Working on "someday" projects that never ship
- Lack of peer feedback or community

## Common Hiring Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### Mistake #1: Confusing Hype with Capability

❌ "They published a paper" ≠ "They can ship code"
✅ Look for people who've shipped real systems, not just research

### Mistake #2: Underestimating the Ramp Time

❌ "A senior engineer should be productive in 2 weeks"
✅ Plan for 6 months for true productivity, 12 months for strategic impact

### Mistake #3: Hiring Without a Clear Problem

❌ "Let's hire an AI expert and see what they can do"
✅ Define the problem, _then_ hire for it

### Mistake #4: Isolating the AI Person

❌ Putting them in a separate "AI team" with no business context
✅ Embed them with product/engineering teams

### Mistake #5: Unclear Success Metrics

❌ "We'll know success when we see it"
✅ Agree on metrics upfront: accuracy targets, deployment timeline, business impact

### Mistake #6: Compensation Misalignment

❌ Offering mid-market rates for senior talent
✅ Benchmark against real market data; accept this is a competitive market
